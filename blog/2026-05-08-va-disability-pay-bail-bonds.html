VA Disability Pay & Bail Bonds: How Veterans Qualify Without a Credit Check

TL;DR — Key Takeaways

  • VA disability compensation counts as income for bail bond installment plans. No credit check needed.
  • The premium is fixed at 10% of the bail amount under CA Insurance Code §1800.4 — never accrues interest.
  • For PTSD/TBI-related charges, Veterans Treatment Court may be available at arraignment under PC §1170.9.
  • Premium installments are typically scheduled around VA pay dates (1st of the month).
  • Service member or veteran families: stable income + qualifying ties to California is what matters, not a FICO score.

Veterans facing a bail situation in California often have one specific question that civilian content does not answer well: does my VA disability pay count as income? The answer at Angels Bail Bonds is yes — VA compensation is treated the same as W-2 employment or Social Security for the purpose of qualifying a veteran cosigner on a bail bond installment plan. No credit check is required. The premium is fixed at 10% under California Insurance Code §1800.4 and never accrues interest.

This post covers how the qualification process works for veterans, what charges commonly bring veterans into the bail process (and why), and how Veterans Treatment Court can be a downstream option at arraignment. Read the TL;DR above for the short version.

How VA Disability Pay Qualifies a Veteran Cosigner

Angels Bail Bonds underwrites premium installment plans on income stability, not credit history. The Department of Insurance prohibits interest on the bond premium itself, so the underwriting question is "can this household reliably make the monthly payment?" — not "what is the credit score?"

VA disability compensation answers that question well. It is:

  • Federally guaranteed. Monthly payments arrive on the 1st of each month from the U.S. Treasury.
  • Documentable. A VA award letter, eBenefits screenshot, or recent direct deposit shows the rating and amount.
  • Generally non-garnishable. 38 U.S.C. §5301 protects VA disability compensation from most creditors, which removes a category of risk most lenders worry about.
  • Predictable. Annual COLA adjustments are public; the monthly amount only changes once per year.

For installment-plan qualification, what matters is that the monthly VA disability amount comfortably covers the scheduled installment with margin for living expenses. A 50% rating veteran receiving roughly $1,200/month easily supports a $300/month installment on a $1,800 premium. A 100% rating veteran receiving roughly $4,000/month supports much larger premiums.

Veterans Treatment Court and PC §1170.9

California Penal Code §1170.9 is the statute that allows courts to consider service-related conditions in sentencing for eligible offenses. The veteran or their attorney requests a §1170.9 evaluation at or after arraignment. If the judge grants it, the case may be diverted to a Veterans Treatment Court, which typically combines:

  • Treatment for PTSD, TBI, MST, or substance use through VA Healthcare or community partners
  • Regular court check-ins (often weekly or biweekly)
  • Mentor pairing with another veteran going through the program
  • Potential dismissal of charges or reduced sentence on successful completion

Eligibility is fact-specific. Felony domestic violence is usually excluded. Misdemeanor and non-violent felony cases involving substance use, mental health crisis, or service-connected conditions are most commonly accepted. A licensed criminal defense attorney with veteran experience is the right resource for evaluating eligibility — Angels Bail Bonds is not a law firm.

Veterans Treatment Courts operate in: Los Angeles, Orange, San Diego, San Bernardino, Riverside, Sacramento, Ventura, Santa Clara, Alameda, and several other California counties as of 2026. Each county runs its own program with slightly different intake criteria.

The Bond-Out Process for a Veteran Family

The mechanics are the same as any California bail bond:

  1. Booking finishes at the city PD or county jail (typically 2-4 hours).
  2. Cosigner reaches out to Angels Bail Bonds. VA award letter or recent deposit screenshot speeds the underwriting.
  3. Indemnity agreement signed. Cosigner takes on the contingent liability for the full bail amount if the defendant skips court — the same as any cosigner.
  4. Premium plan agreed. Down payment + monthly installments scheduled around VA pay dates. No interest. No credit check.
  5. Surety bond posted at the holding facility. Release typically completes in 4-8 hours at city jails, 8-24 hours at county facilities.
  6. First court appearance scheduled. Defense attorney evaluates Veterans Treatment Court eligibility under PC §1170.9 if applicable.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does VA disability pay count as income for bail bonds? Yes. It's treated as stable verifiable monthly income equivalent to W-2 employment or Social Security.

Do veterans need good credit to post bail? Generally no. Angels writes bonds on income, employment or VA disability, residency, and California ties — not FICO.

What charges commonly bring veterans into the bail process? DUI, DV, drug possession, criminal threats. The charge follows the standard CA bail schedule.

Is Veterans Treatment Court available? Yes, under PC §1170.9. Many California counties run programs. Eligibility is determined at arraignment.

Does a service-connected PTSD diagnosis lower bail? Sometimes, at arraignment. Initial booking bail follows the schedule; reductions are argued at the first court appearance.

How do veteran families typically pay the 10% premium? No-interest installments scheduled around VA pay dates (1st of month). Total stays at exactly 10% of bail.
